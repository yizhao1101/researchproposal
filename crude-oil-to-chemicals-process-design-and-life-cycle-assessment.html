4 Crude Oil to Chemicals: Process Design and Life Cycle Assessment

Master thesis

Autumn 2022

4.1 Context

Most refineries focus on producing transportation fuels, which accounts for 50% of total refinery products today. However, with the rapid development of deployment vehicles (EVs) and the increasing decarbonization requirement of the transport sector, the demand for transportation fuels is expected to drop significantly. Recently, EU officially banned the sale of internal combustion engine (ICE) cars from 2035, marking the end of an old era.

Another important product of refinery is naphtha, which is the feedstock for petrochemical industry. The demand for chemicals, such as plastics, fibers and other polymers, is positively related to the GDP level and keeps growing in the last several decades. Therefore, it is foreseeable that the focus of the refinery production will gradually shift to chemicals.

Refiners will have to act in respond to the product spectrum change. One possible solution is the integration of refinery and petrochemical processes, that is, producing chemicals directly from crude oil instead of from the naphtha, and at the same time reducing the production of transportation fuels. However, the direct conversion from crude oil to chemicals (COTC) requires a large retrofit of existing refinery facilities and receive doubts of its environmental benefits.

In this project, the student will investigate the design of COTC processes. The focus will be on the life cycle assessment (LCA). The transition from current refineries to the future ones will be particularly emphasized considering the huge capital investment required for the oil and gas industries. The student is expected to obtain an overall understanding of the chemical industry from this project.

4.2 Literature Study

COTC projects have already received a lot of attentions and a number of COTC plants have been planned in China or the Middle East. A representative project is Hengli’s refinery factory for producing Paraxylene, which achieved a full trial operation in May 2019. As shown in Figure 4.1, the central process of the COTC is still the distillation unit, while various cracking processes are used to crack the long carbon chains into short ones. Reforming process is for the generation of aromatics in the product. The announced investment cost for this project reached 11.6 billion dollars (“Crude Oil-to-Chemicals: Future of Refinery” 2019).

There are also other COTC factories operated by Saudi Aramco aiming for ethylene and propylene production, for which steaming cracking is integrated to produce light hydrocarbon gases.

Regarding to the environmental impact, a large quantity of research works give particular emphasis on chemical production from biomass and renewable electricity. However, there is not a clear statement of the environmental impact of COTC process. Further literature studies are required.

4.3 The missions

This project will be centered on the question “Will crude oil still be needed in the future to produce chemicals?”. It will be organized in the following steps:

  • Conduct an exhaustive literature review for the COTC processes.

  • Do the process design for COTC and optimize the process by heat integration.

  • Conduct cost and LCA for this process.

  • Compared the COTC process with the production of chemicals from biomass and renewable electricity.
